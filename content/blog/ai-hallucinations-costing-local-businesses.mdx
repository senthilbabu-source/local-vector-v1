## "We couldn't figure out why Tuesday nights were dead"

A restaurant owner in Austin noticed a sharp drop in Tuesday evening traffic. Reviews were good. Prices hadn't changed. Location was the same. After three weeks of puzzling over it, a friend mentioned something: "ChatGPT says you're closed on Tuesdays."

They weren't. ChatGPT had pulled outdated information from a 2023 Yelp review that mentioned the restaurant used to close on Tuesdays — two years ago. The AI stated it as current fact.

This is an **AI hallucination**: when an AI model presents incorrect information about a real business as if it were fact.

## How hallucinations happen

AI hallucinations about local businesses aren't random. They follow predictable patterns:

- **Stale data synthesis** — AI combines information from different time periods without noting the difference
- **Source conflict resolution** — when your website says one thing and Yelp says another, AI picks one (often the wrong one)
- **Inference errors** — AI "fills in" missing data by guessing, sometimes incorrectly
- **Training data lag** — models are trained on data that may be months or years old

## The five most common hallucination types

### 1. Operating status
"This business is permanently closed" or "temporarily closed" when you're fully open. The most damaging hallucination — users don't even consider visiting.

### 2. Hours of operation
Wrong opening/closing times, wrong days of operation. Customers arrive when you're closed, or don't come when you're open.

### 3. Services and offerings
"This salon doesn't take walk-ins" when you do. "This dentist only sees children" when you see all ages. Incorrect service descriptions filter out potential customers.

### 4. Pricing and positioning
AI pulls pricing from third-party delivery apps (which include markups) and presents them as your actual prices. Or it describes a premium restaurant as "budget-friendly" based on a single review.

### 5. Location confusion
AI conflates your business with a nearby competitor, combining reviews, hours, or attributes from both into a single (incorrect) answer.

## The revenue math

Consider a restaurant with an average ticket of $45 and 200 covers per week. If an AI hallucination diverts even 5% of potential customers:

- **10 lost covers per week** at $45 average = **$450/week**
- **$1,800/month** in lost revenue
- **$21,600/year** from a single hallucination

For higher-value services like dental or legal, the numbers are even worse. A dentist losing one new patient per week to a "not accepting new patients" hallucination could lose $50,000+ annually.

## What makes hallucinations persistent

Unlike a bad Google review that stays in one place, AI hallucinations propagate. When one model gets it wrong, the error can spread:

1. AI model states incorrect information
2. Content aggregators pick up the AI-generated content
3. Other AI models train on the aggregated content
4. The error reinforces itself across the AI ecosystem

This is why **monitoring matters more than one-time fixes**. Correcting a hallucination today doesn't prevent it from recurring when models retrain.

## How to fight back

1. **Detect hallucinations early** — monitor what major AI models say about your business weekly
2. **Fix at the source** — update the data sources AI models draw from (GBP, Yelp, your website)
3. **Add structured data** — give AI crawlers machine-readable facts instead of making them guess
4. **Monitor for drift** — previously-corrected errors can resurface when models retrain
